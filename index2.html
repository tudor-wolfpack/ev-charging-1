Part 1 · Validation prototype
Today's call

Six decisions to confirm

Each one is quick, and each one directly shapes design, the seed data or the week-1 plan. Tap an option to mark where we land.
1

Payment moment: pay after the session, or hold funds before it?

The current design charges M-Pesa when the session ends. Real EV flows often place a hold before energy is delivered and capture the final amount afterwards. For validation, either works - but it changes the payment sequence your testers experience and the states we design. If a hold is preferred, a deposit or wallet-style pre-charge may be the practical pattern on M-Pesa.

2

QR codes: printed at the stations, or shown in-app for the test?

Affects the scan flow and what we need from your side. If the client prints codes at the physical stations, we generate them; if the test is fully off-site, an in-app simulated code is simpler.

3

Vodacom sandbox access - who is our contact?

Sandbox credentials (or a merchant contact at Vodacom) are the single most timeline-sensitive dependency: we need them in week 1. We would also confirm together what the sandbox supports, since that ties into decision 1.

4

Connector types and tariff model for the seed data

Which charger types (AC / DC) and which pricing model (per-kWh vs per-minute) should the 2-3 seeded stations reflect? Matching the end-client's real intended mix makes the validation more convincing.

5

Branding for the test build

A neutral test brand, or the end-client's brand? This affects the design week and, later, store metadata. If white-label resale (C4) is on your roadmap, a neutral base with theming placeholders is the natural starting point.

6

Which charger hardware is the end-client planning to deploy?

Nothing to build this phase - but knowing the vendor or shortlist tells us which protocol (OCPP version or a vendor API) the simulated engine should mirror, so the later real integration is a bounded swap instead of a rebuild.

SOW proposal

Seven things to confirm for the proposal

These let us structure the proposal precisely - which options we quote, what we assume, and what we flag as dependencies.
7

How do the two tracks connect?

Is the validation prototype a first step inside the SOW journey (in which case we architect it API-first, against the SOW's extensibility rules, so it carries forward) - or a separate internal deliverable for Qualiteq, independent of the vendor selection?

8

Which options should we quote?

Option A is the required baseline. B and C are invited but optional, with components itemised so each compares against Qualiteq's own estimate. Which would be genuinely useful to see from us?

9

How is Qualiteq set up on the middleware components?

Under A and B, our front end consumes your OCPP middleware, pricing engine and settlement APIs. Knowing how your engineering team is organised on these - in-house build, partner, or still being decided - tells us where our proposal adds the most value and what assumptions to write down.

10

Identity & wallet: what exists today?

The SOW keeps this service with Qualiteq under every option, as the foundation for future modules. Is there an existing service (with an API contract we can design against), or is it greenfield? And should the validation prototype already consume it, or use its own throwaway auth?

11

API contracts and sandbox: when can they be published?

The SOW requires whoever owns each backend component to publish an OpenAPI contract and a sandbox before front-end build begins. For the components Qualiteq owns, a realistic publication date is the single biggest input to our Phase 1 timeline - we will flag it as a dependency either way.

12

Payment aggregator: preference and ownership

Selcom, DPO/Pesapal, Flutterwave, or open to our proposal? And who holds the contractual relationship with the aggregator - the end-client, Qualiteq, or the vendor? This shapes the Bank of Tanzania compliance confirmation the SOW asks for, and it interacts with pre-authorisation support on mobile money.

13

Support & SLA: coverage expectation

The SOW defines a 30-minute response for critical incidents and asks vendors to propose a coverage model. Before we price the 12-month support term, it helps to know whether the expectation for the 6-station pilot is business-hours or 24/7 coverage - the cost difference is meaningful and we would rather quote the right one.

Logistics to confirm before we close: the exact proposal deadline (one week from receipt of the SOW - we would like to fix the date), submission by email to Lakruwan Senevirathne, and the agreed channel for written questions during the SOW period.
